# Honda B-Series

## Overview
The Honda B-series is a family of inline-4 engines produced from 1988 to 2001 in the Civic, CR-X del Sol, Integra, and CR-V. Displacements range from 1.6L (B16) to 1.8L (B18) to 2.0L (B20). Famous for VTEC variable valve timing on performance variants (B16A, B18C, B16B). All are dual overhead cam, aluminum block and head, 16-valve, with timing belt (interference design). The B-series is the backbone of the Honda tuner scene — parts interchange extensively within the family.

## Known Issues
- **Timing belt failure = catastrophic** — Interference engine. Broken belt bends valves on all B16/B18/B20 engines. Replace every 60,000 miles or 5 years.
- **Distributor O-ring leak** — The O-ring under the distributor base hardens and leaks oil onto the heater hoses. Looks like a valve cover leak but oil drips from behind the distributor.
- **Valve cover gasket seepage** — Honda uses rubber grommets for valve cover bolts. Bolts loosen over time, gasket hardens, oil seeps onto exhaust manifold (smoke + smell).
- **Idle Air Control (IAC) valve clogging** — Carbon buildup in the IAC passages causes erratic idle and stalling. Common in high-mileage engines.
- **VTEC solenoid screen clogging** — Small mesh screen on the VTEC solenoid traps metal debris, preventing VTEC engagement. Engine feels sluggish above 5,500 RPM.
- **Water passage corrosion in head** — B-series heads have small coolant passages near exhaust ports. Electrolysis and old coolant erode the passages, causing localized overheating.

## Common Procedures

### Timing Belt Replacement
1. Set engine to TDC cylinder #1 — crank pointer aligns with dash mark; cam gear "UP" arrow points UP.
2. **WARNING: This is an interference engine.** Do NOT rotate crank or cams independently once belt is off.
3. Remove crank pulley (134 ft-lb, requires Honda holder tool or impact).
4. Remove timing covers (upper, middle, lower).
5. Loosen tensioner, remove old belt.
6. Inspect water pump for seepage or impeller wobble. Replace as preventive measure.
7. Install new belt with manufacturer marks aligned to cam and crank dots. Pointer at TDC.
8. Tension: Release tensioner spring, belt should have ~1/2 inch deflection between cam gear and water pump.
9. Hand-turn crank TWO full revolutions, recheck TDC alignment. Must be perfect.
10. Reassemble, torque crank pulley to 134 ft-lb.

### Valve Lash Adjustment (Solid Lifter Engines)
1. Engine must be cold (room temperature).
2. Remove valve cover. Organize bolts and grommets.
3. Cylinder firing order: 1-3-4-2. Adjust in pairs at TDC compression:
   - TDC #1: adjust intake 1, 2 and exhaust 1, 3
   - Rotate crank 180°: adjust intake 3, 4 and exhaust 2, 4
4. Feeler gauge specs: intake 0.006–0.007 in, exhaust 0.007–0.008 in (B16A; check year).
5. Loosen locknut, adjust screw, retorque locknut to 9 ft-lb.
6. Recheck with feeler after tightening — locknut adjustment changes the gap slightly.

## Special Tools
- Honda crank holder tool (19 mm hex, prevents rotation during pulley torque)
- Feeler gauge set (lash adjustment)
- Valve adjusting tool (thin screwdriver + 10 mm open-end wrench)
- Timing belt tension gauge (optional — finger test works)

## Common Mistakes
- **Rotating crank with belt off** — Bent valves instantly. Always keep cams and crank synchronized.
- **Using GL-5 gear oil in manual transmission** — GL-5 attacks brass synchros. Honda MTF or GL-4 only.
- **Mixing green coolant with Honda Type 2** — Sludge forms, passages clog.
- **Overtightening valve cover** — The aluminum cover cracks around bolt holes. 7 ft-lb feels finger-tight.
- **Forgetting the balance shaft belt** (if equipped) — Separate small belt behind timing belt. If it breaks, no immediate engine damage but vibration increases.
