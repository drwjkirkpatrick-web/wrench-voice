# Chevy Small Block V8

## Overview
The Chevrolet small-block V8 family includes the 305 (5.0L), 350 (5.7L), and 400 (6.6L) cubic inch displacements produced from 1955 to 2003 in cars, trucks, and marine applications. Gen I (1955–1992) and Gen II (1992–1997, LT-series) are mechanically similar. All share the classic 4.4 inch bore centers, wet sump oiling, and distributor ignition. Gen I uses two-bolt or four-bolt mains. Gen II adds roller cam, one-piece rear seal, and reverse-flow cooling. This is the most swapped engine in history — parts are cheap, knowledge is everywhere, and aftermarket support is unlimited.

## Known Issues
- **Rear main seal leak (Gen I two-piece)** — The rope or neoprene seal behind the crank flange leaks oil. Replacing requires removing the oil pan and loosening main cap #5. Messy job. Gen II one-piece seal is much easier.
- **Intake manifold gasket leak (coolant crossover)** — The intake gasket coolant crossover passages seep externally and internally. Dexcool coolant accelerates the leak (orange sludge). External leak drips down front and rear of block. Internal leak causes coolant in oil.
- **Oil pressure sender failure** — Sender reads erratically or pegs high. Located behind intake manifold near distributor. Replacement requires contortionist hands.
- **Distributor gear wear** — The distributor gear meshes with cam gear and wears. Misfire at high RPM. Bronze gear on steel cam = sacrificial. Replace both gear and cam gear if worn.
- **Piston slap (cold start, all Gen I/II)** — Carbon buildup and bore geometry cause piston-to-wall noise when cold. Goes away when warm. Usually harmless above 50k miles.
- **Hydraulic lifter collapse / tick** — Dirty oil or stuck lifter causes rhythmic tick. Seafoam treatment sometimes works. Persistent tick = replace lifters and inspect cam lobes.
- **Spark plug thread repair** — Aluminum heads (some years) strip plug threads. Helicoil or Time-Sert required.

## Common Procedures

### Rear Main Seal Replacement (Gen I)
1. Remove transmission or lift engine slightly. Remove oil pan.
2. Remove rear main cap (#5). Pry old seal halves from block and cap.
3. Clean grooves thoroughly. Install new seal halves with lip facing IN (toward crank).
4. **CRITICAL:** Apply RTV at the parting line between seal halves AND where the block meets the cap. This is where all rookie leaks happen.
5. Torque cap to 70 ft-lb (or spec for your year).
6. Reassemble pan, refill, check for leaks.

### Intake Manifold Gasket Replacement
1. Drain coolant below intake level.
2. Remove all accessories, distributor (mark rotor position), fuel lines, wiring.
3. Loosen intake bolts in reverse torque sequence (outer to inner, crisscross).
4. Lift manifold straight up. Scrape old gasket material from head and block surfaces.
5. Inspect coolant crossover passages for erosion. Some engines benefit from blocking off internal coolant passages and using external hose if leakage is chronic.
6. Use fel-pro print-o-seal or comparable. Apply RTV at front and rear intake sealing rails.
7. Torque in three stages center → ends: 10, 20, 30 ft-lb.
8. Reassemble. Prime oil system before start.

## Special Tools
- Harmonic balancer installer/puller (cross-bar type, not jaw puller — damages balancer)
- Piston ring compressor (for top-end rebuilds)
- Cylinder ridge reamer (if honing, remove ridge first)
- Helicoil or Time-Sert kit (plug thread repair)
- Degree wheel + dial indicator (performance builds)

## Common Mistakes
- **Installing seal lip backwards** — Leaks immediately. Lip faces inward (toward oil).
- **Forgetting RTV on intake rails** — Fel-Pro gaskets don't seal at the front/rear China walls without RTV.
- **Not marking distributor position** — Reinstall off a tooth = no-start or rough idle.
- **Using jaw puller on harmonic balancer** — Destroys the rubber bond. Use a cross-bar puller.
- **Wrong spark plug thread repair insert** — Helicoil can back out under heat. Time-Sert is more permanent for aluminum heads.
