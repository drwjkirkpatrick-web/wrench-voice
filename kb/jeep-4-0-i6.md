# Jeep 4.0 Inline-6

## Overview
The Jeep 4.0 liter inline-6 (AMC 242) was produced from 1987 to 2006 in the Jeep Wrangler (TJ/YJ), Cherokee (XJ), and Grand Cherokee (ZJ/WJ). Descendant of the AMC 258 (4.2L), the 4.0 is an overhead valve, cast iron block and head design with a bulletproof reputation. Fuel injection (MPI) from 1991 onward with Chrysler-era electronics. Timing chain driven. No distributor on 1996+ (coil rail / coil-on-plug). The 4.0 is famous for running 300,000+ miles with nothing but oil changes. It is arguably the most reliable gasoline engine ever built in America.

## Known Issues
- **Cracked cylinder head (0331 casting)** — The 2000–2001 model years used a TUPY head with a casting flaw between valves. Cracks propagate between #3 and #4 combustion chambers, causing coolant loss and rough running. Replace with 2002+ casting or aftermarket.
- **Exhaust manifold crack** — Cast iron manifold cracks at the collector flange or between ports. Very common, especially on right-hand-drive fleet vehicles. Sounds like ticking at cold start.
- **Oil pump drive assembly failure** — The camshaft drives the oil pump via a hex shaft. The shaft strips or the retaining clip fails, causing sudden oil pressure loss. Engine destroys itself within minutes if not caught.
- **Renix-era CPS (Crank Position Sensor) erratic** — 1987–1990 Renix systems use a CPS with poor weather sealing. Moisture causes intermittent stall/no-start. Relocate to drier spot or replace with sealed unit.
- **Cooling system scale** — The 4.0 runs hot in traffic. Scale buildup in radiator reduces capacity. Flush with citric acid every 60k miles in hard water areas.
- **Thrust bearing wear** — Manual transmission YJ/TJ units transmit axial load through crank thrust bearing. Excessive clutch pressure or riding the clutch causes crank walk. Check endplay with dial indicator.

## Common Procedures

### Cylinder Head Replacement (0331 Crack)
1. **WARNING:** Head bolts are TTY (Torque-To-Yield). They stretch during torque and CANNOT be reused. Budget for new bolts.
2. Drain coolant, remove intake, exhaust, valve cover, accessories.
3. Remove head bolts in reverse sequence (outside → in). Discard bolts.
4. Lift head. Inspect block deck for erosion or pitting. Deck should be machined if > 0.003" warp.
5. Clean all carbon from piston tops. Check for valve contact marks (evidence of prior timing failure).
6. Install MLS head gasket with factory coating side UP. Do NOT add sealant unless specified.
7. Install NEW head bolts. Torque in 4 stages per factory sequence: 25 → 50 → 80 → 110 ft-lb.
8. Reassemble. Critical: burp cooling system thoroughly. The 4.0 traps air at the heater core.

### Oil Pump Drive Assembly Inspection
1. Drop oil pan. Remove windage tray.
2. Inspect hex shaft where camshaft engages. Wear or rounding = replace shaft.
3. Check snap ring / retaining clip. If loose or missing, the shaft can disengage.
4. Prime oil system before first start after any disassembly.

## Special Tools
- Torque angle gauge (for TTY head bolts if using angle-torque method)
- Cylinder head stand (heavy iron head, ~65 lbs)
- Feeler gauge set (valve lash on 1987–1990 engines)
- Radiator pressure tester (burping the 4.0 requires pressure to push air out)

## Common Mistakes
- **Reusing TTY head bolts** — Stretched bolts cannot hold spec torque. The head lifts, gasket fails, coolant enters oil.
- **Not burping the cooling system** — Air pockets cause localized boiling, head warpage, repeat failure. Run with cap off, heater on, squeeze upper hose to push air.
- **Wrong spark plugs** — Champion RC12YC or equivalent. Platinum plugs foul quickly in 4.0 due to cold-start richness.
- **Mixing ATF in manual transmission** — AX-15 requires GL-4. GL-5 ruins synchros.
- **Ignoring oil pump drive shaft** — This is the Achilles heel. Ten dollar part, five thousand dollar engine. Inspect it.
